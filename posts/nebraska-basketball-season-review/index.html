A Tale of two halfs the Nebraska basketball seasson

NCAA
Basketball
Nebraska
Author

Brady Rasso

Published

April 13, 2022

After the best season in the Fred Hoiberg era, Nebraska basketball entered the year with expectations of returning to the Big Dance. With a newly assembled roster through the transfer portal and key players returning, it seemed like there was no way this team wouldn’t at least make a push for an at-large bid. After a first half that set them up for success the rest of the season, we saw a completely different team down the stretch—one that not only destroyed the Huskers’ tournament dreams but also failed to qualify for the Big Ten tournament. So, what happened between the first and second half of the season?

The Huskers were one of the streakiest teams in the country, with the ability to get hot and torch opponents from three, going on long win streaks—or go ice cold and struggle to take care of lesser competition, leading to multiple consecutive losses. This inconsistency didn’t just apply to game results, but to other aspects of their play as well. Nebraska lived and died by the three a season ago—but how did that carry over?

We could clearly see a drop-off in three-point production from the first half to the second half of the season. And when the moments were the brightest, the shots from behind the arc just weren’t falling. So, with a team that Coach Hoiberg built around size—featuring bigger players at nearly every position—did that size actually translate into rebounding production for the Cornhuskers? More importantly, how did that impact their ability to close out defensive possessions with rebounds or create second-chance opportunities on offense?

We did see some improvement in rebounding during the second half of the season, but at best, Nebraska was average when it came to controlling the glass. And being just average in that area—especially with the size this roster had—isn’t going to cut it when it’s time to win big games. So what does Fred Hoiberg need to do in the portal to make a real run at the Big Dance next year? Find quality shooters—and bring in dominant bigs who can consistently clean the glass.
